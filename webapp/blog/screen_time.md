## Recommended Limits at a Glance
<a name="recommended-limits-at-a-glance"></a>

| Age | Major medical bodies | Practical translation |
|-----|----------------------|-----------------------|
| 0–18 months | **American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)** – video‑chat only; no entertainment screens<sup>1</sup> | Stick to face‑to‑face time—even if it’s through a camera. |
| 18–24 months | Co‑view only *high‑quality* shows<sup>1</sup> | A single well‑produced educational episode watched *together* beats passive cartoons. |
| 2–5 years | ≤ 1 h day of non‑educational media<sup>1</sup> | One episode, then outside to play. |
| 6 + | “Consistent limits” & no screens robbing sleep, activity, or relationships<sup>2</sup> | Set firm off‑hours (meals, pre‑bed), keep devices out of bedrooms. |
| Under 5 (WHO) | < 1 h day total; preferably less<sup>3</sup> | Treat screen time like dessert—occasional, measured. |

> **Why no single hard limit after age 6?**  
> Evidence shows *quality, timing, and context* matter at least as much as raw minutes.

## What Too Much Screen Time Does
<a name="what-too-much-screen-time-does"></a>

| Domain | Key findings | Sample evidence |
|--------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Sleep & circadian rhythm** | Blue‑light exposure in the 2 h before bed delays melatonin, cutting total sleep by ≈ 30 min<sup>4</sup> | Preschoolers’ melatonin suppressed **≈ 70 %** by dim light in the hour before bedtime. |
| **Physical health** | > 2 h day recreational screens **doubles** obesity risk vs < 1 h<sup>5</sup> | Sedentary time displaces active play and promotes mindless snacking. |
| **Eyesight (myopia)** | Each extra daily screen hour → ≈ 21 % higher odds of nearsightedness; risk nearly doubles beyond 4 h<sup>6</sup> | Outdoor daylight is the antidote—aim for 2 h outside daily. |
| **Mental health** | Heavy leisure use (> 4 h day) links to more anxiety, depressive symptoms, and reduced self‑esteem<sup>7</sup> | Mechanism: social comparison + disrupted sleep + less offline interaction. |
| **Language & executive function (under 5)** | Background TV lowers parent–child verbal exchanges by > 15 %<sup>1</sup> | Screens can crowd out the conversations toddlers need. |

Bottom line: **Screens become a problem when they displace sleep, movement, outdoor light, reading, or real‑world relationships.**

## Evidence‑Based Playbook for Managing Screen Time

1. **Guard the bookends of the day**  
   *No screens during the hour after wake‑up or the two hours before bed.* Sleep and mood improve measurably when families enforce this window.<sup>4</sup>

2. **Prioritise content over clock‑watching**  
   Educational, co‑viewed programs show neutral‑to‑positive outcomes, while algorithm‑driven short‑form videos are tied to attention problems.<sup>7</sup>

3. **Match device to activity**  
   Homework on a laptop? Fine. Doom‑scrolling short‑form video in bed? Hard no. Teach a quick *purpose check* before unlocking.

4. **Keep screens out of bedrooms—at every age**  
   Kids with bedside devices sleep ~8 % less and report 50 % more daytime fatigue.<sup>5</sup>

5. **Design the physical environment**  
   A family charging station in the kitchen + screen‑free dining table + books or sports gear within reach make choosing offline activities easier.

6. **Model the behaviour you want**  
   Children of parents who exceed 4 h day phone use are **2× more likely** to do the same.<sup>7</sup>

7. **Use tech tools—but don’t outsource parenting**  
   OS‑level controls (iOS Screen Time, Google Family Link) are brake pedals, not autopilot. Combine them with a written family media plan (free templates from the AAP).

## Red Flags Worth Your Attention

* Frequent eye strain, headaches, or blurred distance vision.  
* Mood crashes when the device is removed.  
* Screen use creeping into meals, bathrooms, or hidden late‑night sessions.  
* Outdoor play < 60 min day.

Any **two** of these together justify tightening limits or consulting a professional.

<br/>
<br/>

## Key Takeaways

* **Under 5s:** treat screens like sweets—small, high‑quality portions, co‑viewed.  
* **School‑age & up:** aim for balance; protect sleep and outdoor play; enforce no‑device zones.  
* **Quality beats quantity**, but once entertainment use regularly exceeds ~4 h day, negative outcomes rise sharply.  
* Use a family media plan, model healthy habits, and keep the offline world tempting.
